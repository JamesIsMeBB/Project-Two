Saving Youth

It Takes a Peer-Led Effort...

Read More What's Been Done

Arizona high schools – where teen dating violence typically begins – have an astounding lack of school enacted preventative measures. 7.6 percent of high school students reports physical dating violence in Arizona and 8.6 percent of Arizona high school students also reports sexual violence (CDC, 2019). Even schools with mitigation tactics or victim resources still do not meet what should be requisite: prevention.

It should be noted that youth dating violence is also not a problem that victims can dependably overcome themselves; it is not a problem that will simply be grown out of for perpetrators nor is it a problem contained within a vacuum. Youth dating violence has led to the death of 227 youth under the age of 24 from 2008 – 2018. People die because of this. Without prevention, youth dating violence perpetuates itself as “…engagement in violence is a strong predictor of future violent behaviors. For example, youth who are both victims and perpetrators of bullying are at an increased risk for delinquency and violence over time” (Vivolo-Kantor et al., 2021). Violence once is a sign of violence again. People don’t just grow out of solidly established behaviors but rather they linger like bad habits and stay low to the ground of the mind. This issue takes sturdy, thorough, preventative, immediate action if we ever hope to prevent the violence in our schools and upon our youth, if we hope to save lives.

Other groups in Arizona, such as Bloom365, have begun the realization within the state that youth dating violence requires more than mitigation but requires a personal involvement of the peers within the context of the issue (Bloom365, 2021). Unfortunately – though this is difficult – these groups have yet to largely break into breeding grounds for a majority of the teen dating violence: high schools. If the counselors of GUHSD give teens the opportunity work together, a powerful and influential change can be made across the youth.

The Issue

Arizona’s youth suffer from the social plague known as youth dating violence. This has been a recognized problem from the Arizona government itself; Governor Doug Ducey declared February “Teen Dating Violence Awareness and Prevention Month,” with supporting statements such as “…teen dating violence crosses race, gender, and socioeconomic lines,” (Governor’s Office of Youth, Faith and Family, 2020).

Non-existent prevention in the Arizona high schools is the precursor to rampant youth dating abuse where at-risk teens are allowed to perpetrate or be victimized. This lack of uniform prevention is why this proposal will speak directly to the counselors of the Glendale Union High School District on establishing an afterschool club for prevention of youth dating violence within high schools. This club will be geared towards at-risk teens – though anyone may join to participate – with a focus on peer-led education, discussion, leadership, and engagement.

At a Glance

The solution is a peer-led (peers meaning peers of the age group and peers involved in the solution) club where peers can lead research, education, activities and discussion. This club should be sponsored by a high school counselor willing to endorse the club and facilitate meetings. The club should be open to all but any potential or active victims or perpetrators of youth dating violence (at-risk) should be referred into the club by the school counselors. The club should be engaging and eye opening for all participants through open discussion, relevant education, and participatory activities. Lastly, members should be encouraged to be mindful of club education and apply what they can to their lives.

Why a Club?

A first question that may is, “Why do we need a club to begin with?” Journal of School Health research asserted that, “Given that few youth seek help on their own, schools might create more formalized reporting mechanisms to ensure such violence is addressed effectively and efficiently, and create peer and parental support networks to build awareness and create comfort for victims” (Lachman, Zweig, Dank, Yagner, 2019). The youth of high schools, including GUHSD (Glendale Union High School District), are not speaking up on this violence and this is from an obvious absence of peer-support networks.

The CDC also recognizes that youth dating violence requires early education that can serve as prevention when they say, “During the pre-teen and teen years, it is critical for youth to begin learning the skills needed to create and maintain healthy relationships. These skills include knowing how to manage feelings and how to communicate in a healthy way” (2020).

A Peer-Led Approach

By far, the most vital component to this prevention program is a peer-led approach. This solution advocates for peer-led discussions and research and education and more, but why? According to the Journal of School Health, “Previous research has shown that teen dating violence victims are most likely to seek out informal sources of help, especially their friends and parents” (Lachman et al.,2019). In addition, this same research indicates that a sweeping 77.2 percent of victims sought help from friends and only 16.8 percent. Any other sources to which victims may report to fall far below the

above percentages (Lachman et al., 2019). This simple research presented is a blatant sign of what is occurring when victims of youth dating violence seek help: they confide in peers. From here a leading question should be asked, “How can we incorporate a solution that involves peers and ensures peers are informed to take the proper and responsible actions to help?”

Education

The answer is education, education that is served over time and education that deals out information on self-respect, healthy relationships, and other useful tools toward prevention. The education must be engaging, respectful, researched, and most importantly, peer-involved. Prevention Science holds research on a CDC developed group, Dating Matters®, which incorporated education as a key role as well. According to this research, “Results demonstrated that both male and female students attending schools implementing Dating Matters reported 11% less bullying perpetration and 11% less physical violence perpetration than students in comparison schools” (Vivolo-Kantor et al., 2021). Education proved widely successful and the implementation was, “…repeated exposure to these lessons over the 3 years of middle school…” which further supports the argument of early adaptation of education over time (Vivolo-Kantor et al., 2021). Education can’t be served out periphrastically to the entirety of a school in an impersonal one-time assembly of sorts. Another group from Prevention Medicine was the ERSG program; this program gives more reason to personal education over time as its “…curriculum includes five multi-session units…” and throughout, “There were incremental reductions in reactive and proactive aggression, psychological and sexual perpetration, and psychological, physical, and sexual victimization…” (Reidy, Holland, Cortina, Ball, Rosenbluth, 2017).

It must be emphasized that the education must be peer-driven if it ever wishes to have an impact that is valuable. Additional support for a peer-led education that is developed and given through peers is through the Journal of Family Violence on the Start Strong Boston group. It was demonstrated that “The program utilizes peer-led education and youth empowerment strategies to engage adolescents in all aspects of program development and implementation. These methods were chosen to deepen participants' acceptance and retention of healthy relationship messaging topics” and this approach was widely engaging and feasible (Beatriz et al., 2018). The reasoning behind this novel approach was given, “By including Boston adolescents in every part of the programming, from planning to implementation to evaluation (the focus of the present manuscript), the program goes beyond talking about adolescents, to talking to them and with them. Having Peer Leaders be part of the solution to the very serious issue of teen dating violence benefits both them and the participants in the program” (Beatriz et al., 2018). It is clear that education, over time, which is peer-led and engaging, serves as a robust catalyst to change in youth dating violence.

Alternatives

“Let’s just have an assembly with the entire student body at once and get the education across quick and easy,” or “Let’s just make a class for this sort of thing.” Both of these solutions lack the central component of success: peer-led solution. Without addressing every issue of each suggestion, these alternatives will already prove to be weak through their lack of peer-involvement. It has already been proven above that peer-involvement plays an instrumental role in successful youth dating violence prevention. Apart from this, an assembly does not engage students nor does it cement any knowledge as it does not expand its education out over enough time. The class alternative could prove successful to some extent – depending on design – but is much more difficult to incorporate into a GUHSD high school then a club. All-in-all, a club is streamlined, cheap, customizable, engaging, and peer-led; all-in-all a club is the most effective solution.

In Closing

The wonderful part of all of this is the personalization that can be had so that a solution may be tailored specially for every high school student body. Peer-led research, club development, discussions, and activities are facets for customization. The club can hold the interest of that particular student body and work for that particular school as the club, its organization, operations, education, and activities can all be done in a way that fits best. So long as the central ideas of education and peer-led advocacy are sustained, the club can prove to be vastly more successful than any mitigation tactic.

Counter Arguments

“This solution can’t stop active violence, there will always be someone who falls through the cracks,” “Peer-led and developed groups will not be completely possible,” or “The club will not be able to get any funding on its own.” These concerns are incontrovertibly valid and deserve to be addressed.

Futile

It’s true, no club can wholly stop all ongoing violence in the moment and there may always be at least one victim, at least for some time after implementation. Though it should be noted that this proposal was never made in hopes that all other mitigation tactics and help resources should be eliminated. This club must work in conjunction with other resources, mitigations, and intervention. In tandem, a vast improvement could be obtained but missing any piece will cause failure. This proposal is a piece to the larger puzzle.

Impossible

This club will solve the puzzle through a peer-led mindset that could be asserted as not feasible, reasonable, or possible. Earlier the Start Strong Boston group was examined; this group was the most pervasive of all other examined groups in peer-involved development, mentoring, and leadership. The research conduct on Start Strong Boston clearly stated, “…we demonstrate that the inclusion of youth Peer Researchers in the design and conduct of the evaluation of an after-school based TDV prevention program for middle-school youth is both acceptable and feasible” (Beatriz et al., 2018). It may seem easy to call the youth of high schools incompetent and it may be easy to assume their failure, but it takes a change within the system to bring about long lasting resolution. Start Strong Boston proved the validity of peer-structured movements and it is up to us to implement the best solution with powerful impacts.

No Funding

If nothing else, the solution could fall short if it intersects with logistical obstacles such as a lack of funding. Luckily, this solution is low cost and funding is the least of the worries. Education, research, discussion, and activities are all free or extremely low cost. With a touch of creativity and dedication, this solution won’t require any set number of resources that aren’t immediately available to any GUHSD high school. Again, comes the aspect of customization of this solution. This solution can be developed and tailored to the needs and capabilities of the school no matter the situation.

As a counselor, simply consider this solution. Consider the benefit that could be had across the board. Consider sponsoring a prevention club and facilitating club meetings. Consider seeking out at-risk youth who could join and consider seeking youth who could stand up as leaders as well. Consider spreading awareness yourself to the student body through the attached advertisement on the school bulletin boards.

There are simple steps that can be taken as a counselor to get the club off the ground: sponsor and initialize a prevention club, seek out students who would be interested in being initial peer leaders, spread the For Friends advertisement and put together a list of at-risk teens, then hold the first meeting. The first meeting and onward will involve working with all members to build a curriculum of what is important to them, what their issues are, and what will benefit them the most. Along with this, other club operations and activities can and should be discussed. Your peer leaders (all members should be encouraged to lead education and or discussion at one point or another) will be your indispensable partners in helping the students you care for. This will take time and patience but these actions are a basis for success.

As a counselor – in your position who cares for their students – it becomes your responsibility to advocate for their well-being just as much as it is their own responsibility. Do you accept this responsibility and with it the power to save lives?
